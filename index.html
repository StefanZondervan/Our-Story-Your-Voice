Introduction

This interactive map, guided by the methodology of Waldo Heilbron, is a journey through Rotterdam’s colonial heritage. It emphasizes accountability, inclusive narratives, and community engagement to reframe history. The map first explores the port's institutional connections. It then guides you through different types of heritage—tangible, intangible, and mental—using specific examples from Rotterdam. This is a starting point. The journey ends with a platform for collective heritage where you are invited to add your own stories and perspectives, building a diverse archive of historical narratives.

Institutional Representation

The Port of Rotterdam shapes historical narratives through its institutions, including museums and heritage departments. These institutions emphasise economic growth and technological advancement while downplaying controversial aspects of the role of colonial history.

Tangible Heritage

This encompasses physical elements of history like monuments and artefacts. In Rotterdam's maritime institutions, tangible heritage largely focuses on items connected to economic elites and colonial "glory," primarily representing white historical perspectives.

Intangible Heritage

This encompasses non-physical elements like stories, traditions, and oral histories. It holds particular importance for descendants of enslaved people and remains vital for marginalised communities whose histories are often absent from traditional institutions.

Mental Heritage

Mental heritage encompasses invisible social contexts, structures, and generational trauma that historically oppressed enslaved people and continue to influence modern institutions and systems. It includes the collective memory and psychological impact of historical events that persist in today's society.

Collective Heritage

This platform serves as a growing archive of diverse historical narratives, allowing everyone to contribute their stories and perspectives, ensuring a more inclusive and complete understanding of history.

Stadsarchief Rotterdam

The city archives contain extensive historical documentation about Rotterdam's port development, though access and interpretation of these materials primarily reflect institutional rather than diverse community perspectives.

Maritime Museum

The Maritime Museum, primarily presents Rotterdam's maritime history through economic achievements while minimising colonial aspects. While exhibitions have traditionally focused on white elite perspectives, recent efforts show slow progress toward including marginalised voices.

Portlantis

Portlantis, established by the port authority, focuses solely on economic output and innovation. This new museum initiative exemplifies current institutional approaches that highlight technological and commercial achievements while not mentioning its historical context.

Tax & Customs Museum

The museum's exhibitions focus on historical revenue collection, including controversial topics like the opium trade. While highlighting economic aspects, the museum understates the human cost and colonial exploitation that enabled such trade.

FutureLand

A visitor center showcasing the port's expansion projects and technological innovations, emphasising future developments while maintaining the traditional narrative of economic progress.

VOC Marine warehouse

A preserved structure from the Dutch East India Company era, embodying both the technological achievements of Dutch maritime trade and Rotterdam's complex colonial legacy.

Statue of Piet Hein

The Statue of Piet Hein, erected on October 17, 1870, stands as a monument to Dutch maritime history. Piet Hein commanded several colonial expeditions, and his statue glorifies these actions and colonial past.

Sint Jobshaven

Sint Jobshaven represents significant tangible heritage through its connection to colonial history, particularly with the arrival of repatriates from the Dutch East Indies in the 20th century. The nearby Lloydkade, named after the Lloyd shipping company that operated colonial trade routes, reflects this colonial past in its name.

Leuvenhaven

A historic harbor area representing Rotterdam's maritime heritage, featuring preserved vessels and infrastructure that celebrate the technological and commercial achievements of the port's past.

Boompjes

A historic waterfront location crucial to Rotterdam's maritime trade, now transformed into a modern business district while preserving elements of its maritime heritage.

Kapsalon

The Kapsalon is a modern example of blending cultures, created in 2003 by Nataniël Gomes, a Cape Verdean hairdresser in Rotterdam. This fast-food dish blends fries, döner meat, cheese, and salad, representing a new culinary tradition born from the combination of different cultural foods.

Summer Carnaval

The Summer Carnaval in Rotterdam is an celebration of the diverse cultures of Latin America and the Dutch Caribbean and its communities within the Netherlands. This annual event, centered around a street parade, not only brings these traditions to the Netherlands but also serves as a platform for cultural exchange and community celebration.

FENIX Museum

The museum presents migrant stories through objects, audio, and visual representations, offering an inclusive narrative by employing people with diverse backgrounds and lived experiences.

Rotterdam Airlines Music Group

Rotterdam Airlines Music Group shows Rotterdam's significant cultural role in Dutch urban music. The label has evolved from a local talent platform into a major cultural force. Through its record label Rotterdam Airlines has created a community of young rappers, giving a voice to a new generation and establishing a new cultural tradition.

Afrikaanderwijk Riots

The 1972 Afrikaanderwijk riots arose from tensions between Turkish migrants and Dutch residents amid rising unemployment. In response, the government enacted the "Spreidingsbeleid" policy, which limited the percentage of minority groups in certain neighborhoods, effectively creating racial segregation.

Descendants

Many descendants of enslaved people in Rotterdam experience a form of displacement that shapes their identity. Neither feeling fully Dutch nor completely connected to their ancestral homelands, they live in a state of "in-betweenness"—a mental heritage passed down through generations.

The Benefits Scandal

The "toeslagen affaire" demonstrates persistent colonial thinking in Rotterdam. From 2013-2019, tax authorities discriminated against people with non-Dutch surnames when processing benefits applications, revealing how institutional racism continues in modern administrative administrative systems, causing lasting trauma in marginalized communities.
